Global Climate Change, explained

There is no question that the planet we live on is changing, but what is causing this alteration, why is it not happening the same everywhere and how can we stop it?

As you walk outside in your heavy coat and see the snow fall in late February, it is hard to believe that our planet is actually warming at all.

Climate Change is a phenomena experienced differently everywhere

Rates of warming are not constant accross different locations. Certain areas are experiencing warming at a faster rate than others, such as the Arctic region which is warming twice as fast as other regions.

The seas are rising too, and quickly

A major concern associated with global climate change is the rising of sea level. Sea level rise is mainly a result of the melting of glaciers due to increased temperatures.

This will stop soon though, right?

Graphic about how this will continue going forward

Unless we take action soon, this problem will continue to worsen. While it is hard to estimate exactly what we expect to happen, a range of models have made predcicitons for future conditions. Within 100 years, CO2 emissions could reach anywhere between 500-900 ppm, temperatures could increase anywhere between 2 and 6 degrees Celsius, and sea levels could rise anywhere between 2 and 4 feet. These changes could have catastrophic implications including increased occurance of natural disasters, complete flooding of coastal cities, extinction of species, decline of crop health and the overall end of life as we know it. It is too late to completly halt all of the negative effects of climate change but it is not too late to help mitigate some of these impacts. Reducing our greenhouse gas emissions will have a direct impact on how damaging these impacts may be.

You can make a difference

While it may be easy to lose hope, there are simple changes you can do to really make a big differnce. A study by MIT found that the average carbon footprint for an average US citizen is 20 metric tons, while the world average is a mere 4 tons. This means our country is a huge contributer to the problem, but there are many steps you can take to help reduce that. Traveling less by gasoline powered vehicles, eating local foods, switching to renewable energy sources, composting and reducing water use are all easy alterations to your daily routine that can scale up and make a big difference.
