Global Climate Change, explained

There is no question that the planet we live on is changing, but what is causing this alteration, why is it not happening in the same way everywhere and how can we help stop it?

As you walk outside in your heavy coat and see the snow fall in late February, it is hard to believe that our planet is actually warming at all. "Global warming" refers to the average annual temperature increase, something that may not be felt the same way in every region due to varied climates. While Earth as a whole is warming, certain regions may even be cooling. When you think about a huge system such as global climate change, you will not be able to understand what is happening when you just think of one pinpoint location.

Global climatic patterns are analyzed for long term patterns because they have been relatively constant through Earth's history. The problem that we are facing now however, is that each decade has been warmer than the last since 1960, and the most recent three decades have been the warmest we have on record. This unprecedented rate of heating is due to the emission of greenhouse gasses, corresponding with the industrial revolution. Almost every thing we do from driving to work to eating meat to cutting down a tree have a negative impact on our atmosphere. So, while you may be turning up the thermostat more than usual, there is a real global crisis occuring that needs to be addressed before it has the chance to get any worse.

Climate Change is a phenomena experienced differently everywhere

Rates of warming are not constant accross different locations. Certain areas are experiencing warming at a faster rate than others, such as the Arctic region which is warming twice as fast as other regions. This is a result of systems accumulating energy at the poles, causing increased warming. Also, as ice melts, it lowers the ability to reflect the sun's energy back into space. This creates a negative feedback loop where more warming leads to more melting which in terms leads to more warming.

The greenhouse gas problem

There is no doubt that greenhouse gas concentrations have been increasing in our atmosphere. Recently, greenhouse gas concentrations reached the highest amount in the last 800,000 years. These greenhouse gas concentrations are altered slightly by climatic shifts such as El Nino and La Nina years, but the clear general trend is a dramatic rise in these concentrations. Greenhouse gases consist mainly of Carbon Dioxide, Methane and Nitrous Oxide. This huge surge in greenhouse gas concentrations is largely a result of human-induced factors such as population increase, agriculture, deforestation, industrialization and fossil fuel emssions. While natural process remove some greenhouse gases from the planet, our current rate of emissions far exceeds any rate of removal. Since the liftime of emissions in the atmosphere is increadibly long, we are on track for a devestating amount of warming.

The planet is actually warming, and fast

There have been significant heat increases experienced in the recent past. Since the industrial revolution, the global temperature has increased by 0.8 degrees Celsius. While that may not seem like much, over two thirds of this increase has occured in the last 50 years alone. So while the temperature changes themselves are not huge in magnitude, it is the rate that is so alartming. With nothing stopping it, this temperature increase will increase into the future and cause irreversible damage to our way of life.

The seas are rising too, and quickly

A major concern associated with global climate change is the rising of sea level. Sea level rise is occuring for several reasons. As the temperature of water increases, the water expands. About half of sea level rise to this point is an effect of this thermal expansison. The rest comes from ice melting in the form of glaciers and land ice. This is a real cause for concern becuase in the United States, almost 40 percent of the population lives in coastal regions that are in severe danger of the erosion, flooding and storm events that accompany sea level rise.

This will stop soon though, right?

Unless we take action soon, this problem will continue to worsen. While it is hard to estimate exactly what we expect to happen, a range of models have made predcicitons for future conditions. Within 100 years, CO2 emissions could reach anywhere between 500-900 ppm, temperatures could increase anywhere between 2 and 6 degrees Celsius, and sea levels could rise anywhere between 2 and 4 feet. These changes could have catastrophic implications including increased occurance of natural disasters, complete flooding of coastal cities, extinction of species, decline of crop health and the overall end of life as we know it. It is too late to completly halt all of the negative effects of climate change but it is not too late to help mitigate some of these impacts. Reducing our greenhouse gas emissions will have a direct impact on how damaging these impacts may be.

You can make a difference

While it may be easy to lose hope, there are simple changes you can do to really make a big differnce. A study by MIT found that the average carbon footprint for an average US citizen is 20 metric tons, while the world average is a mere 4 tons. This means our country is a huge contributer to the problem, but there are many steps you can take to help reduce that. Traveling less by gasoline powered vehicles, eating local foods, switching to renewable energy sources, composting and reducing water use are all easy alterations to your daily routine that can scale up and make a big difference.
